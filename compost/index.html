Overview 

Aim

This design will look at different composting systems and how they could fit into the site to create an effective composting process.

Date

November - December 2020

Location

Wibsey Community Gardens, Wibsey, Bradford, West Yorkshire, U.K.

Status

Context

Wibsey Community Gardens is on a 2.5 acre site that was previously the Bradford Council Parks Dept shrub nursery. It was leased to Bradford Community Environment Project (BCEP) until they folded and the site was taken on by Bradford Organic Community Services in 2017. I was employed BOCS in 2020 as community gardener. The site has three commercial polytunnels and also a large number of raised beds in the open ground. The key objective of my role was to grow produce.

I saw the need and possibility of producing compost, to support the fruit and vegetable production.
A lot of organic materials are available for use on the site. It would be possible to recycle them and turn them into high quality compost to support the growth of healthy plants. There is potential to create different types of compost for different purposes.
The current composting system is not working. It has been neglected and doesn't function effectively. There are many opportunities for composting waste that are being missed.

Survey

What are the priorities?

  • To create high quality compost to support food production

What are the additional things it would be nice to have?

  • to train staff and volunteers how to use the compost systems
  • To run composting workshops for staff, volunteers and visitors
  • Food waste collection/donation from local community

What is the vision for the site?

  • To be community-sufficient in compost
  • To have efficient, low maintenance systems 
  • To have a straightforward system that processes all waste from the veg production areas
  • For the systems to be close to where waste is produced

There is a small budget available for the overall growing project and some of it can be used for the composting systems. Investments made in composting systems will result in savings in the future as it will reduce or even prevent the need to buy in compost.

Timescale - It would be beneficial to start developing the compost systems straightaway, so as to reduce the need for buying in compost next year.

The different composts will take between six months and two years to cure. 

Why is compost needed?

There are 144 raised beds on the site. A wide variety of food crops are grown in these, to be sold to visitors, cafes and shops. The growing media in the beds is depleted as crops have been harvested but they have not been adequately topped up with compost, if at all. The beds need topping up as a matter of urgency. In the first year, it will be necessary to top up with bought-in compost but this is expensive and results in increased carbon emissions. 

Healthy soil means healthy crops that are more resilient to pests and drought. Healthy soil also yields crops with higher nutritional content. 

Why a redesign?

The current system is not effective because;

  • The current bays are too far from most of the working areas
  • Workers don't know what to put where so they dump everything on The Heap
  • The compost bays are not safe as the paths are slippery and there is run-off pooling on the paths 
  • Grass clippings from the bowling green in the park are being used. It is likely that these contain chemical treatments such as pesticides and herbicides
  • The current bays are wooden and are rotting
  • There are bits of timber lying around, some with nails in
  • The Heap is now home to nesting birds, foxes and possibly hedgehogs and more
  • There is very little compost being made on site, and a lot is needed
  • The volunteer who was maintaining the compost bays could not continue due to the pandemic
  • Workers have little knowledge about composting

What is working well?

  • There are some good paths in place

Climate and landform

In choosing what to survey, I thought about the design and it's requirements. I wanted to make sure I was only surveying that which was pertinent to the design. For example, there was no point in doing soil tests because nearly all of the growing at this stage is done in raised beds.

Climate does affect composting to a point, but in the climate of West Yorkshire, the effects are negligible.

Rainfall is significant throughout the year.

Prevailing winds are from the west. As the site is on a hill, it is a windy site and the average windspeed is 19 km/h. Gusting speeds can be as high as 90km/h (worldwideweatheronline.com)

Sector analysis

I decided that sector analysis wasn't necessary for this design, it was enough to know which way the prevailing winds came from so that strong winds do not damage any systems put in place.

Flood zones were considered as building a compost system in an area that floods could cause run off pollution and nutrient loss as well as slowing down the composting process.

Frost is not usually an issue when making and using compost as it isn't generally used in winter and systems are adequately covered to protect against the elements.

Drought will not be significant enough to affect the composting systems and use thereof.

Sun mapping is not required.

Soil

The majority of the crops are grown in raised beds, so soil testing would not have been appropriate. There are a large number of beds and they have been topped up with various substrates at different times, so each bed would have had to have been tested, which would not be a sensible use of resources.

  • Large amount of organic matter not being composted effectively
  • Current composting systems are too far from most activity
  • Flooding

Limiting factors  - non-physical

  • Woodchip from local arborist not being used (apart from for paths)
  • Local council can provide leaves in autumn but connections are not there
  • Workers are not learning about composting
  • There are not enough workers
  • There may be limits of what level of composting can be undertaken - research
  • Staff may not have time to maintain systems and train volunteers and other staff
  • Management, workers and volunteers don't understand why it's important to make compost

Functions, systems and elements

 

The key functions of the composting system are; 

  • Maintaining a closed loop
  • Nutrient cycling
  • Supporting plant growth
  • Increasing biodiversity
  • Soil carbon sequestration
  • Low maintenance
  • Water saving 
  • Weed reduction

I considered which elements performed which functions

 

I thought about how the inputs and outputs were processed.

Goals

Needs

  • make enough compost to meet needs on site to be community sufficient
  • close the loop of organic material being wasted/processed inefficiently
  • create low maintenance systems
  • make sure that weeds are composted effectively so that weed seed and roots are killed in the process
  • design durable systems - sustainability is met through implementation of ethics
  • place systems where they are needed

Aims for the future/separate designs

  • ensure workers are safe  - this is part of the clean-up project
  • provide workers with clear guidance on how to use systems - this is part of communication and training project
  • ensure future gardeners understand the system - this is part of communication and training project

Maintenance

To a certain extent, using the systems is a major part of their maintenance. As one function for the systems was 'low maintenance', as a whole the system should be fairly low maintenance.

I wrote a basic maintenance plan. It is only basic because I did not stay in that job.

Evaluation

In-depth evaluation was not possible for this design as it wasn't implemented. But as far as I can tell, if the design had been implemented, the SMART goals would have been achieved.

Tweak

Looking back at this design I can imagine that it would have been necessary to build a composting system in the old raised beds area. I chose not to put one there as I wasn't sure how much capacity we needed for the outdoor raised beds waste and I wanted to make sure that the one-turn system had enough material. 

I would hope that we would have found a way to produce ramial wood chip. As the forest garden project was a long way from being implemented and using rwc in vegetable beds can be tricky, it was maybe for the best. 

I suspect that some systems would have performed better and that those may have replaced ones that didn't do as well. But having incorporated a divers range of systems means that the best fit would have become evident over time.

Availability of inputs may have changed such as if the tree surgeons stopped providing free wood chip or we may have set up a community composting system. Developing systems for these changes would have been necessary

Principles

In addition to those mentioned in the design decisions video, below are the ways that the principles come into use 

Catch and Store Energy: By developing systems that collect resources when they are abundant, we can use them in times of need.

In closing the nutrient loop by gathering organic materials and recycling them into compost, what would have been lost has been put to best use. 

Obtain a Yield: Ensure that you are getting truly useful rewards as part of the work that you are doing.

This is a key principle for this design. The main yield is compost, and it a invaluable resource for the broader project. The efficiencies integrated into this design will mean that the yield is increased at little cost and with myriad benefits.

Use & value renewable resources & services: Make the best use of nature’s abundance to reduce our consumptive behaviour and dependence on non-renewable resources.

Another key principle, the design is energy and resource efficient, using resources that are on site or free. By making our own compost, we are closing the loop, and reducing use of peat, synthetic fertilisers, perlite, plastics and fossil fuels.

Using salvaged materials where possible to build the compost systems reduces the need for purchasing new materials that have a carbon footprint.

Using willow and hazel for ramial wood chip  means that a resource that is truly renewable is being put to good use, and as using it actually increases the resource and reduces growth that is problematic, the benefits are numerous.

Produce no waste: By valuing and making use of all the resources that are available to us, nothing goes to waste.

By valuing all plant material, including so-called pernicious weeds, nutrients and organic matter are cycled and a yield results. By using coppiced willow (some of which are unwanted), hazel and other trees, something that is otherwise problematic is put to excellent use. In using woodchip in efficient systems, the mycorrhizal process produces highly nutritious compost. 

Integrate rather than segregate: By putting the right things in the right place, relationships develop between them and they support each other.

Choosing the right place for each compost system, in relation to it's use and functions means that they can function all the better and will not be problematic. Placing systems in the correct zones means that they are more likely to be maintained and used effectively.

Use and value diversity: Diversity reduces vulnerability to a variety of threats and takes advantage of the unique nature of the environment in which it resides.

By using a variety of systems, we will produce a variety of composts, with different properties for different uses. We will be supporting a diverse flora and fauna, which in turn will support the wider environment, such as the parks and gardens beyond the site.

Ethics

Earth Care
Earth Care is very much at the forefront of this design. The soil of this ex-council nursery has been depleted but can be regenerated through the introduction of large amounts of compost, and the resulting increased biodiversity. The following practices are also in line with Earth Care;

  • reducing use of bought in compost 
  • not using peat-based composts
  • using only organic methods (no synthetic fertilisers or herbicides)
  • cycling nutrients effectively
  • providing excellent habitat for microorganisms
  • providing a diverse food source for birds and invertebrates
  • making use of waste materials

People Care and Fair Share
This design will support the broader project, for which people care and fair share are integral.
