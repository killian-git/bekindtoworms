Overview

Aim

To create a functional, accessible kitchen garden that will allow people of all abilities, physicalities and ages to enjoy growing edible plants.

Date

23.06.21 - 18.08.21

Location

West Yorkshire, U.K.

Client

Private

Status

To be implemented

Principles

Land/Nature stewardship

Framework

GoSADIM

Goal setting, Survey, Analysis, Decision Making, Implementation, Manage
Then the additional steps of Evaluate & Tweak

Tools

PASTE, DAFOR, sector analysis, overlays

Resources/support

Miro, Suncalc.org, Pinterest, Google Earth

Context

This design is about creating a functional and accessible kitchen garden to be used by a community for learning, connecting with nature, socialising and having fun

The client wanted to install a kitchen garden that would be the focus for activities for community groups.

This was the extent of the brief.  The client left it to myself and the company I was working with to create a garden that we could develop into a sustainable business.

I decided to use this opportunity to create my dream kitchen garden, and one that would be accessible to as many people as possible. I decided to use materials that had the least ecological impact and to include elements that would benefit all aspects of the environment, where possible.

The site for the kitchen garden is within a larger area designated for a bigger community gardens project. This in turn is on a very large site. There are many logistical details that need to be addressed, but these are not in the remit of this design

 

Goal articulation

What I want.

I would like to create a garden that;

  • produces a wide variety of fruit and vegetables
  • increases biodiversity
  • provides opportunities for people can come to learn and connect with nature/the growing process
  • is accessible for everyone and can engage a diverse range of people
  • is not only sustainable but also regenerative
  • strengthens the local community and is shaped by the local community
  • looks beautiful, to inspire and engage
  • uses environmentally friendly and organic methods - reducing waste, recycling/reusing. free from pesticides, herbicides, no peat, no dig, less fossil fuels (hand tools rather than power tools)
  • uses local resources, especially waste from on site, e.g. woodchip, leaves and grass clippings

Analysis

What the limiting factors and potential resources are.

Through analysis, our challenge is to design and establish a system where the energy needed to maintain it decreases over time, while its outputs increase  - Aranya, Permaculture Design

Limiting factors


Physical limiting factors
There is no water catchment at present.
The irrigation system is limited to one tap, which is by the wall to the east. This is a long way from where the kitchen garden has been sited.
Deer have access to the site via the car park entrance.
Squirrels, rabbits and pigeons are likely to damage crops
There are several areas that may be problematic for accessibility for those using wheelchairs/other mobility aids - different levels, trip hazards, broken paths etc.

Non - physical limiting factors
There are many unknowns - funding, community engagement logistics, local residents' reactions, communication systems.

There has been no consultation with the local community. 

There are many separate parties involved. We do not know each other very well, and the communication system is long-winded. Communication is disjointed, objectives are unclear etc.

We do not own the land.

We do not know if the design will be implemented.

At present, we do not have a tenure agreement.

No local people have been involved in this project as yet.

Decision Making

What I need to change.

Kitchen garden placement

The decision of where to place the kitchen garden was straightforward. The area next to the greenhouse previously had raised beds, so some paths are already in place. Also, it is close to the greenhouse. Putting it anywhere else would have been far more complicated and resource heavy. Use small and slow solutions #Permaculture Principle

There had been some discussion about developing the whole walled garden site. I came up with a rough idea of what could be done and this can be seen below;

There is the potential to expand the kitchen garden further, at a later date

Raised bed layout

I had to decide on the layout of the raised beds. I tried a few different configurations that can be seen here

The layout that I chose is made up of a pattern of seven beds that is repeated twice. If founding doesn't allow, just one set could be built. 

With two sets, there were two areas left to fill where plants could be grown. I decided a herb garden would be a simple element in one of the areas. Herbs will provide a crop and also food and habitat for wildlife, including pollinators. I decided to plant green manures in the other area to suppress weeds and enrich the soil. This could be developed later to grow other plants, perhaps more herbs. 

Soil Improvement systems placement

Having looked a the options I decided that all of the systems would work well on the site, as they all utilised waste that was readily available, and I anticipated that we would need a lot of compost. 

The siting for the systems changed, but the original placement can be seen here.

Other decisions

I decided to use hazel obelisks and arches to make use of the vertical, for growing crops such as peas, sweet peas and other things. 

I chose two areas for bug hotels. 

I placed the seating area by the greenhouse on an area that is fairly flat and will have a nice view of the raised beds.
