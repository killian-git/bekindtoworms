Overview

Aim

To create a functional, accessible kitchen garden that will allow people of all abilities, physicalities and ages to enjoy growing edible plants.

Date

23.06.21 - 18.08.21

Location

West Yorkshire, U.K.

Client

Private

Status

To be implemented

Domain

Land/Nature stewardship

Principles

The Design Principles, from David Holmgren in Permaculture Principles & Pathways Beyond Sustainabilty 

Framework

Then the additional steps of Evaluate & Tweak

Context

This design is about creating a functional and accessible kitchen garden to be used by a community for learning, connecting with nature, socialising and having fun

The client wanted to install a kitchen garden that would be the focus for activities for community groups. There is no kitchen for the garden to serve at present. 

This was the extent of the brief.  The client left it to me to create a garden that could be part of a sustainable business and part of a bigger project that utilised the gardens on the larger site. 

I decided to use this opportunity to create my dream kitchen garden, and one that would be accessible to as many people as possible. I decided to use materials that had the least ecological impact and to include elements that would benefit all aspects of the environment, where possible.

Local consultation was not possible, but I would hope that this would take place at some point, within the design of the larger project. 

Goal articulation

What I want.

I would like to create a garden that;

  • produces a wide variety of fruit and vegetables
  • increases biodiversity
  • provides opportunities for people can come to learn and connect with nature/the growing process
  • is accessible for everyone and can engage a diverse range of people
  • is not only sustainable but also regenerative
  • strengthens the local community and is shaped by the local community
  • looks beautiful, to inspire and engage
  • uses environmentally friendly and organic methods - reducing waste, recycling/reusing. free from pesticides, herbicides, no peat, no dig, less fossil fuels (hand tools rather than power tools)
  • uses local resources, especially waste from on site, e.g. woodchip, leaves and grass clippings

Analysis

What the limiting factors and potential resources are.

Through analysis, our challenge is to design and establish a system where the energy needed to maintain it decreases over time, while its outputs increase  - Aranya, Permaculture Design

Limiting factors


Physical limiting factors
There is no water catchment at present.
The irrigation system is limited to one tap, which is by the wall to the east. This is a long way from where the kitchen garden has been sited.
Deer have access to the site via the car park entrance.
Squirrels, rabbits and pigeons are likely to damage crops
There are several areas that may be problematic for accessibility for those using wheelchairs/other mobility aids - different levels, trip hazards, broken paths etc.

Non - physical limiting factors
There are many unknowns - funding, community engagement logistics, local residents' reactions, communication systems.
There has been no consultation with the local community. 
There are many separate parties involved. We do not know each other very well, and the communication system is long-winded. Communication is disjointed, objectives are unclear etc.
We do not own the land.
We do not know if the design will be implemented.
At present, we do not have a tenure agreement.
No local people have been involved in this project as yet.

Decision Making

What I need to change.

Kitchen garden placement

The decision of where to place the kitchen garden was straightforward. The area next to the greenhouse previously had raised beds, so some paths are already in place. Also, it is close to the greenhouse. Putting it anywhere else would have been far more complicated and resource heavy. #smallandslowsolutions #patternstodetails

There had been some discussion about developing the whole walled garden site. I came up with a rough idea of what could be done and this can be seen below;

There is the potential to expand the kitchen garden further, at a later date

Raised bed layout

I had to decide on the layout of the raised beds, using the dimensions defined by my accessibility research. I tried a few different configurations that can be seen here

The layout that I chose is made up of a pattern of seven beds that is repeated twice. If funding doesn't allow, just one set could be built. 

With two sets, there were two areas left to fill where plants could be grown. I decided a herb garden would be a simple element in one of the areas. Herbs will provide a crop and also food and habitat for wildlife, including pollinators. #Obtain a yield I decided to plant green manures in the other area to suppress weeds and enrich the soil. This could be developed later to grow other plants, perhaps more herbs. 

Soil Improvement systems placement

Having looked a the options I decided that all of the systems would work well on the site, as they all utilised waste that was readily available, and I anticipated that we would need a lot of compost. 

The siting for the systems changed, but the original placement can be seen here.

When I came to drawing up the design, I realised that there was a better place for the composting systems, near the greenhouse door where a path already exists - this would make the composting system accessible straight away. #integrateratherthansegregate

Other decisions

I decided to use hazel obelisks and arches to make use of the vertical, for growing crops such as peas, sweet peas, etc.  

I chose two areas for bug hotels. #obtainayield 

I placed the seating area by the greenhouse on an area that is fairly flat and will have a nice view of the raised beds.

The gutters of the greenhouse are quite low down, so small water butts will be installed to collect water.

The tap shown on the basemap is the only source of irrigation, so a hose system will be set up from this.

Permaculture Principles

Observe and interactThrough doing a survey I learnt a lot about the site and noted some issues that needed addressing. This helped me create a realistic budget and will help to calculate how long tasks will take to complete, should I get to the stage of completing the work plan.
Catch and store energyThe composting systems do this nicely. 
Obtain a yieldThe garden will provide opportunities for learning and connection with natural processes, whilst also providing food and habitat for wildlife
Apply self-regulation and accept feedback

Although creating a kitchen garden where the focus isn't about growing crops may seem strange, it is actually appropriate to the mother project. If the mother project goes ahead, the kitchen garden will provide many benefits. If the mother project doesn't go ahead, the kitchen garden can still function as a discrete system.

Taking on the role of stewardship of this land means that I have a responsibility to the wildlife community as well as human community, so I have included as many regenerative solutions as possible, such composting systems, water catchment, permeable paths and raised beds made of sustainable materials.

Use and value renewable resources and services

By making as much compost on site as possible, the need for buying in from elsewhere is reduced. 

By using a water catchment system, the need for using mains water is reduced

Produce no wasteA diverse range of composting systems means that all the organic waste from the larger site can be utilised efficiently and create something of high value
Design from patterns to detailsPlacing the kitchen garden where raised gardens had been previously meant that the systems already in place, such as paths and the greenhouse, would support the new garden.
Integrate rather than segregateRe-thinking the placement of the three bay compost system meant that paths that were already there would provide greater accessibility
Use small and slow solutionsMake the least change for the greatest effect - by placing elements where existing infrastructure existed meant that energy and other resources could be used elsewhere. 
Use and value diversityBy making the site accessible to all, a diverse range of people will be able to come and interact with the garden. Diversity is at the heart of this project, that aims to be of benefit to as many people and creatures as possible.
Use edges and value the marginalA constraint of this design is that it cannot yet show how this garden will fit in with the other elements of the bigger project. There will be a good deal of edge. 
Creatively use and respond to changeThe pandemic has meant that more people want to be outside, there is an increased demand for outdoor activities, and hopefully these needs can be met in part by this project. 

Reflection on the design process

What I learnt about permaculture through the design process
I learnt a new framework.

I am starting to see that there is skill in selecting what to survey and to what level of detail to record data.

I feel as though I'm getting the hang of what is a function, a system and an element - this really baked my noodle but by going through it methodically, it started to make sense.

Through having not done a sector analysis, I started to see it's purpose!

What I learnt about myself through the design process
I recognise that I wasn't methodical enough. I realised that in rushing ahead with ideas meant that I missed important steps out, steps that would have facilitated deeper thought processes. I went back over things where I could, but it was too late to make changes to the final design. To be fair to myself, the deadline was a tight one and I didn't really have time for much deliberation. Once I have completed the diploma, those processes should be well imbedded into my thought processes so that I use them automatically.

How this design process has helped me
It has helped me to see the process I would follow with sufficient time. Looking back at it now, it has made me think of other opportunities that are on the site, such as water catchment on a big scale, and incorporating rain gardens.

What went well?
I realised that I should have done a client interview. As it would have been like shutting the gate after the horse had bolted, I looked at the goal articulation and defined the functions from this. It was a bit of a bodge job, but it worked just fine. I was pleased that I had come up with a solution...a solution for a problem when looking for a solution for a solution...oh dear, I feel like I'm in the Matrix.

What was challenging?
I thought that using a different framework, one that looked a bit simpler, would make the process easier. This wasn't the case. I think that my main difficulty is working out which elements go under which headings, and that is just something that will become clearer as I progress through the diploma. 

There wasn't enough time for prolonged observation

Long term vision and goals

The long term vision is that this kitchen garden is part of a bigger community garden that is able to sustain itself financially.

Next achievable steps

This project is now on hold, so there are no next achievable steps.
