This design will look at different composting systems and how they could fit into the site to create an effective composting process.

Context

The 2.5 acre site was previously the Bradford Council Parks Dept shrub nursery. It was leased to Bradford Community Environment Project (BCEP) until they folded, and the site was taken on by Bradford Organic Community Services in 2017.

A lot of organic resources are available for use on the site. It would be possible to recycle them and turn them into high quality compost to support the growth of healthy plants. There is potential to create different types of compost for different purposes.
Reducing the reliance on bought in compost reduces the carbon footprint of the project and saves money.

This design will also assess the current plans for the installation of thirty new raised beds and eight compost bays, calculating the best configuration and provide plan for the implementation.
In addition to this, the design will look at options for filling and topping up the new beds and topping up all other beds, both those outside and in the polytunnels.

The current composting system is not working. It has been neglected and doesn't function effectively. There are many opportunities for composting waste that are being missed.

Waste is food. All fertility arises from decay. There is no life without death, and death feeds new life. Life and death, decay and regeneration, are part of the same cycle. We cannot have life without death, fertility without rot. But death need not be feared or viewed with horror. It is part of the cycle - a transformation, not an end.

Survey

The client

The client is responsible for a market garden and one of the main aims is to produce a wide range of fruit and vegetables. I identified the potential and need for compost production. There is potential for the site to be community sufficient in compost.

What are the priorities?

  • To create high quality compost to support food production

What are the additional things it would be nice to have?

  • to train staff and volunteers how to use the compost systems
  • To run composting workshops for staff, volunteers and visitors
  • Food waste collection/donation from local community

What is the vision for the site?

  • To be community-sufficient in compost
  • To have efficient, low maintenance systems 
  • To have a straightforward system that processes all waste from the veg production areas
  • For the systems to be close to where waste is produced

There is a small budget available for the overall growing project and some of it can be used for the composting systems.

Timescale - The composting systems need to be put in place straightaway, so as to reduce the need for buying in compost next year. 

Why is compost needed?

There are 144 raised beds on the site. A wide variety of food crops are grown in these, to be sold to visitors, cafes and shops. The growing media in the beds is depleted as crops have been harvested but they have not been adequately topped up with compost, if at all. The beds need topping up as a matter of urgency. In the first year, it will be necessary to top up with bought-in compost but this is expensive and results in increased carbon emissions. #Earth Care

Healthy soil means healthy crops that are more resilient to pests and drought. Healthy soil also yields crops with higher nutritional content. #People Care #Fair Share

Why a redesign?

The current system is not effective because;

  • The current bays are too far from most of the working areas
  • Workers don't know what to put where so they dump everything on The Heap
  • The compost bays are not safe as the paths are slippery and there is run-off pooling on the paths 
  • Grass clippings from the bowling green in the park are being used. It is likely that these contain chemical treatments such as pesticides and herbicides
  • The current bays are wooden and are rotting
  • There are bits of timber lying around, some with nails in
  • The Heap is now home to nesting birds, foxes and possibly hedgehogs and more
  • There is very little compost being made on site, and a lot is needed
  • The volunteer who was maintaining the compost bays could not continue due to the pandemic
  • Workers have little knowledge about composting

What is working well?

  • There are some good paths in place

Climate and landform

In choosing what to survey, I thought about the design and it's requirements. I wanted to make sure I was only surveying that which was pertinent to the design. For example, there was no point in doing soil tests because nearly all of the growing at this stage is done in raised beds.

Climate does affect composting to a point, but in the climate of West Yorkshire, the effects are negligible.

Rainfall is significant throughout the year.

Prevailing winds are from the west. AS the site is on a hill, it is a windy site and the average windspeed is 19 km/h. Gusting speeds can be as high as 90km/h (worldwideweatheronline.com)

Sector analysis

I decided that sector analysis wasn't necessary for this design, it was enough to know which way the prevailing winds came from so that strong winds do not damage any systems put in place.

Flood zones were considered, as building a compost system in an area that floods could cause run off pollution and nutrient loss, as well as slowing down the composting process.

Frost is not usually an issue when making and using compost as it isn't generally used in winter and systems are adequately covered to protect against the elements.

Drought will not be significant enough to affect the composting systems and use thereof

Sun mapping is not required.

Principles

Catch and Store Energy: By developing systems that collect resources when they are abundant, we can use them in times of need.

By gathering organic materials and recycling them into compost, we are making the best use of something that would be a waste product. Especially composting perennials and wood that would otherwise have been burnt.

Obtain a Yield: Ensure that you are getting truly useful rewards as part of the work that you are doing.

By effectively composting a variety of resources from on site and off site, we are gaining a number of yields

Apply self-regulation & accept feedback: We need to discourage inappropriate activity to ensure that systems can continue to function well.

Finding a way to get everyone to use the compost heaps correctly

Use & value renewable resources & services: Make the best use of nature’s abundance to reduce our consumptive behaviour and dependence on non-renewable resources.

By making our own compost, we are closing the loop, and reducing use of peat, synthetic fertilisers, perlite and plastics

Using willow and hazel for ramial wood chip  means that a resource that is truly renewable is being put to good use, and as using it actually increases the resource and reduces growth that is problematic, the benefits are numerous.

Produce no waste: By valuing and making use of all the resources that are available to us, nothing goes to waste.

By valuing all plant material, including so-called pernicious weeds, we are cycling nutrients and obtaining a yield. By using coppiced willow (some of which are unwanted), hazel and other trees, we are making use of something that is otherwise problematic.

Design from patterns to detail: By stepping back, we can observe patterns in nature and society. These can form the backbone of our designs, with the details filled in as we go

By seeing the patterns in people's habits - pathways to drop off compost, habits in how to dispose of waste, it is possible to design efficient systems.

Integrate rather than segregate: By putting the right things in the right place, relationships develop between them and they support each other.

Choosing the right place for each compost system, in relation to it's use and functions means that they can function all the better and will not be problematic

Use and value diversity: Diversity reduces vulnerability to a variety of threats and takes advantage of the unique nature of the environment in which it resides.

By using a variety of systems, we will produce a variety of composts, with different properties for different uses. We will be supporting a diverse flora and fauna, which in turn will support the wider environment, such as the parks and gardens beyond the site.

Reflection

Not identifying aims/goals at the start doesn't feel right, because when you are doing the survey, you need to know what these are - otherwise you might waste time gathering data you don't need

It was suggested to me that I record the length of time that it took to get from one area to another when calculating distances travelled to move compost/garden waste. I think that was a really good idea but I didn't have time to do it. I may do that in future large-scale designs.

In the process of creating SMART goals, I realised that what I thought was a goal was actually how I would do it. Swapping the S's and the A's around helped clarify the goals
